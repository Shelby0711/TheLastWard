# Game Bible

## Working Title: **The Last Ward**

### Tagline: **Knowledge is expensive.**

\---

## 1\. High Concept

**The Lasr Ward** is a 1–4 player co-op first-person horror game set inside an abandoned hospital that was once attached to a hidden orphan ward. A group of friends breaks down on a road trip, takes shelter inside the hospital, and slowly discovers that the building is not merely haunted — it is alive with memory, grief, and something far worse.

The game is built around one central idea: **the more a player learns, the more the Entity notices them**. Players who solve more puzzles, uncover more clues, and lead the group become more hunted. This creates a constant tradeoff between progress and safety.

The horror is psychological, oppressive, and social. The game is not just about surviving a monster. It is about surviving trust, uncertainty, and the consequences of understanding too much.

\---

## 2\. Design Pillars

1. **Knowledge has a cost**  
Players are rewarded for investigation, but the Entity becomes more aggressive toward the most informed player.
2. **Co-op under pressure**  
Players must work together to survive, but the game constantly creates fear, confusion, and leadership tension.
3. **The dead still matter**  
Eliminated players do not become spectators only; they can still help the survivors by watching from one player’s perspective and spotting dangers the living miss.
4. **Horror through implication**  
The game favors dread, aftermath, and unsettling discoveries over gore. The Entity’s brutality is shown through ritualistic displays, environmental storytelling, and disturbing symbolism.
5. **A memorable antagonist**  
The Entity is not a generic monster. It is a stitched, remembered presence shaped by forgotten children, the hospital, and old suffering.

\---

## 3\. Target Experience

* Session length: 20–45 minutes per run
* Player count: 2–4
* Platform: PC first, then mobile-adjacent or mobile if performance allows
* Perspective: First-person
* Tone: Terrifying, tragic, oppressive, occasionally mournful
* Replay value: High, through randomized clue placement, dynamic routes, adaptive Entity behavior, and multiple possible final outcomes

The ideal player emotion curve is:
**curiosity → unease → paranoia → panic → sacrifice → silence**

\---

## 4\. Core Story

Four friends are driving late at night when their car breaks down in the middle of nowhere. After failed attempts to restart the engine, they spot a distant light through the fog: a derelict hospital building on the edge of an abandoned settlement.

The building was once part of a rural care system that concealed unwanted children in a sealed ward. The official story says the place closed after a fire. The real story is buried under contradiction, missing records, and decades of silence.

Inside, the players discover signs of a forgotten orphan ward, a vanished village, and a child who did not fit the world’s understanding. The clues reveal that the Entity is not a simple ghost. It is a remembered presence formed from the suffering, fear, routines, and unfinished voices of the forgotten children who lived there.

The hospital was never fully cleaned out. It was not merely abandoned. It was erased.

Now it remembers.

\---

## 5\. The Entity

### Identity

The Entity is a composite horror presence formed from forgotten children, lost routines, trapped memories, and the hospital itself. It behaves with the unpredictability of a broken memory: sometimes childlike, sometimes violent, sometimes sorrowful, sometimes mocking.

### Personality

* Curious
* Territorial
* Playful in a cruel, unsettling way
* Possessive of the hospital
* Fixated on players who uncover the truth
* Capable of imitation and misdirection

### Core Behavior

The Entity does not simply patrol and attack. It **studies**.

* It watches who solves puzzles first.
* It learns who leads the group.
* It targets players who interact with the most clues.
* It uses sound, illusion, and mimicry to separate players.
* It escalates pressure on the player who has become the most knowledgeable.

### Motto in Design Terms

**Knowledge is expensive** means the Entity treats understanding as a threat. Players who learn too much become the most interesting to it.

### Appearance

The Entity should not have a single stable form. It may present as:

* a childlike silhouette at a distance
* a malformed figure in corridors
* a voice in radio static
* a reflection that does not match reality
* a familiar teammate’s outline or sound

It should feel like something that was never meant to become one thing.

\---

## 6\. Unique Co-op Horror Hook

This game’s co-op identity is built on **living players and dead players having different roles**.

### Living Players

* Move through the hospital
* Solve puzzles
* Avoid the Entity
* Unlock new areas and escape routes

### Dead Players

When eliminated, a player does not enter free-roam spectator mode. Instead, they can inspect one living player at a time and see only that player’s perspective.

Dead players can:

* switch between surviving players
* spot threats the active player misses
* warn about doors, vents, movement, or hidden danger
* help guide the survivors verbally or through limited in-game communication

Dead players cannot:

* freely roam the map
* see everything at once
* solve puzzles directly
* remove all uncertainty

This turns death into a tense support role instead of downtime.

\---

## 7\. Final Objective and Endgame Twist

The Entity is not trying to kill everyone equally. It wants one witness to leave.

The final structure should build toward a last escape point where the players discover that the exit can only truly be completed by one survivor. If multiple survivors remain, the decision becomes theirs: who escapes, and who stays.

This makes the ending socially and emotionally painful instead of purely mechanical.

### Final Outcome Options

* **One survivor escapes naturally** after others have sacrificed themselves.
* **Multiple survivors reach the end**, forcing the group to decide who leaves.
* **No one leaves cleanly**, allowing the Entity to finish its farewell song as the hospital claims the dead.

The final scene should feel like a ritual, not a victory lap.

\---

## 8\. Story Structure

### Act I: Breakdown

* Car breaks down
* Players explore the abandoned road
* They discover the hospital
* Early clues suggest something is wrong, but not what

### Act II: The Ward

* Players find the orphan ward and patient records
* The Entity begins to show signs of learning the group
* Puzzle density increases
* The first player deaths occur
* Dead players begin assisting from the perspective view system

### Act III: The Truth Beneath the Records

* Clues become more subtle and fragmented
* Contradictory documents appear
* The players begin piecing together the forgotten history of the ward
* The Entity becomes more aggressive toward the most knowledgeable player

### Act IV: The Farewell

* Final routes open
* The Entity stops behaving like a roaming threat and becomes a closing force
* The final escape choice is revealed
* The Entity sings the farewell song with the dead teammates

\---

## 9\. Clue and Lore Design

The hospital should tell its story through environmental storytelling and increasingly difficult clues.

### Early Clues

* Patient intake sheets
* Nurse notes
* Child drawings
* Burnt record fragments
* Old intercom messages

### Mid Clues

* Contradictory witness logs
* Maintenance reports
* Hidden ward references
* Audio fragments with missing context
* Smeared writing on walls and doors

### Late Clues

* Subtle contradictions in dates, names, and locations
* Hidden references to the real cause of the hospital’s disappearance
* Notes that hint at the Entity’s memory rather than its origin
* Lore fragments that imply the hospital erased itself from the world

### Lore Rule

The clues should never fully explain everything. They should create a growing, painful sense that the players are getting close to the truth while never fully owning it.

\---

## 10\. Puzzles

The puzzles should be readable, modular, and tied to the hospital theme.

### Puzzle Types

1. **Power routing**  
Restore power to wards, elevators, and security systems.
2. **Key and lock progression**  
Find keys, codes, and physical access items.
3. **Audio reconstruction**  
Piece together distorted tapes or intercom sequences.
4. **Record sorting**  
Match patient files, dates, and room assignments.
5. **Medical system restoration**  
Restart old equipment, valves, and generators.
6. **Environmental routing**  
Move through changing corridors and blocked sections.

### Puzzle Philosophy

* Puzzles must create information pressure.
* Solving puzzles should increase progress and danger.
* High-value clues should attract the Entity more than trivial tasks.
* Puzzles should support cooperation without making the game purely abstract.

### “Knowledge is expensive” in Puzzle Form

The most important clue is often the most dangerous one.
The player who reads deeper, explores further, or decodes more of the hospital’s truth becomes a more attractive target.

\---

## 11\. Player Death and the Message System

Eliminated players’ bodies and remnants are not just decoration. They are part of the game’s communication system.

### Purpose

The Entity uses the aftermath of eliminated players to send messages to the remaining team.

### Message Types

* **Warnings**  
A body or scene placed in a way that points toward danger.
* **False clues**  
A victim’s aftermath may lure the team toward the wrong route.
* **Complaints / traces of the past**  
Notes, posture, or symbolic arrangement can suggest staff negligence, fear, or cruelty.
* **Memory fragments**  
The Entity may stage a victim’s remains or belongings in a way that echoes the hospital’s old history.
* **Mockery**  
The Entity may arrange a body in a way that taunts a player’s role or behavior.

### Important Rule

These displays must remain horrifying and ritualistic without becoming overly graphic for the sake of shock. The fear should come from implication, composition, and the sense that the Entity is communicating with intent.

### Example Messaging Uses

* A surviving player is misdirected by a “helpful” body display.
* A dead teammate’s location hints at a safer route.
* A victim is arranged beside a clue that confirms a past event.
* The Entity “remembers” a player’s obsession and stages them accordingly.

\---

## 12\. Signature Horror Elements

### The Entity’s Mimicry

The Entity can lure players by impersonating distant voices, teammate audio, or familiar sounds. It should never be a perfect, fair copy. It should always feel slightly wrong.

### The Entity’s Violence

The game should show the Entity as terrifying and brutal without relying on gore. The horror comes from how it dominates space, how it stages victims, and how it makes the building itself feel complicit.

### The Farewell Song

At the end of the run, the Entity sings a slow, unsettling lullaby. The voices of the dead teammates can join it, making the ending feel mournful and unnatural.

\---

## 13\. Level Structure

The hospital should be modular and progressive, with each area adding a new layer of fear or puzzle complexity.

### 1\. Exterior / Roadside Entry

* Broken car, fog, sparse light
* Short tutorial area
* Establishes isolation

### 2\. Lobby and Reception

* First navigation space
* Easy clues
* Simple power or access puzzle

### 3\. Orphan Ward

* Core lore area
* Child records, drawings, whispered audio fragments
* More aggressive Entity behavior

### 4\. Treatment Wing

* Medical machinery, locked rooms, maintenance systems
* Higher puzzle density

### 5\. Staff Areas

* Hidden records, sealed offices, contradiction-heavy clues
* Evidence of who knew what and when

### 6\. Burned / Altered Wing

* Environmental distortion, damaged geometry, stronger Entity presence
* The truth starts to become unstable

### 7\. Basement / Service Tunnels

* Navigation-focused survival space
* High tension, low visibility, ambush potential

### 8\. Final Exit Route

* The last stretch
* Forces the final decision
* The farewell song and ending sequence

\---

## 14\. Multiplayer Flow

### Start of Session

* Players spawn together at the broken vehicle
* Basic objective appears: find shelter and restore a way out

### Mid Session

* Players split for clues, keys, and access
* The Entity learns from the most active investigator
* Dead players begin appearing as a support layer if someone dies

### Late Session

* The group realizes the Entity is focusing on knowledge and leadership
* The endgame path opens
* The game shifts from exploration to survival and moral choice

\---

## 15\. Art Style

### Direction

The game should lean toward **3D first-person horror with old-fashioned, low-tech visuals**, similar in spirit to Granny-style presentation, but with its own identity.

### Visual Goals

* Low-to-mid poly 3D environments
* Simple, readable silhouettes
* Slightly rough or dated textures
* Limited modern polish in favor of uncanny retro atmosphere
* Heavy fog, dim lights, and cheap institutional materials

### Visual Tone

The world should feel like:

* a forgotten PS2-era horror space
* a grim old hospital
* a place where visuals are simple but unsettling because of composition and sound

### Color Palette

* Sickly greens
* Faded whites
* Rusted browns
* Dim reds
* Pale emergency lighting
* Dirty yellow flashlights

### Character Design

* The Entity should be visually memorable but not overly detailed
* Surviving players should be readable in dark spaces
* Dead-player aftermaths should look deliberate and symbolic

\---

## 16\. Audio Direction

Audio is one of the most important parts of the experience.

### Needs

* Distant thumping movement
* Uneven breathing and whisper layers
* Childlike song fragments
* Radio static and interference
* Metal creaks, door groans, and building stress sounds
* Sudden silence used as a scare tool

### Audio Design Principle

The Entity should often be heard before it is seen.

### Voice Use

* Teammate communication is vital
* The Entity may mimic voice snippets at low quality
* Dead-player perspective should have subtle audio filtering and distortion

\---

## 17\. UI / UX

The UI should be minimal and functional.

### In-Game HUD

* Health / stamina / sanity-style indicator if needed
* Objective prompt
* Inventory or tool slot
* Small status indicator for player role or perspective

### Spectator UI

* Switch surviving player
* Basic text indication of whose perspective is being watched
* No free camera by default

### Endgame UI

* Final warning / choice sequence
* Simple, ominous, non-intrusive presentation

\---

## 18\. Tech Stack Recommendation

### Engine

**Unity 6 with URP** is the best practical choice for this project.

Why:

* Good for cross-platform development
* Strong first-person support
* Good balance of visual performance and workflow speed
* Easier to scale to PC first and then mobile-adjacent builds if needed

### Multiplayer

Use a modern networking layer such as:

* **Photon Fusion** for fast iteration and hosted multiplayer, or
* **Mirror / Fish-Network-style architecture** if you want more control and lower costs

### Backend / Data

* Local save system for progress and settings
* Simple backend for telemetry, matchmaking, and player accounts if needed
* Cloud storage only if the design later requires cross-device persistence

### Tools

* **Blender** for 3D asset creation
* **Substance Painter** or a lightweight texturing workflow
* **FMOD** or Unity audio workflows for adaptive sound design
* **Git + Git LFS** for version control

### Why Unity Over a Heavier Alternative

This project needs fast iteration, horror-focused lighting, performance control, and a team-friendly workflow. Unity is a practical fit for that.

* Modular AI for the Entity
* Dynamic target selection based on hidden knowledge score
* Perspectival spectator system for dead players

## 20\. Future Expandability

This core game can later expand into:

* New hospital wings
* Different facilities with the same Entity mythology
* Additional Entities or memory layers
* More endings
* Difficulty modes
* Cosmetic-only progression
* Challenge modes with fixed clue layouts
* Variant rule sets for repeat playthroughs

\---

## 21\. Tone Rules

### Do

* Make the Entity feel alive and aware
* Keep clues unsettling and layered
* Reward cooperation
* Let dead players matter
* Make the final choice painful

### Do Not

* Explain everything
* Overuse explicit gore
* Turn it into a pure PvP betrayal game
* Make the Entity feel like a generic monster
* Make death feel like downtime

\---

## 22\. One-Sentence Pitch

**A co-op first-person horror game where four friends get trapped inside a forgotten hospital, uncover a buried orphan ward mystery, and discover that the smartest player becomes the Entity’s favorite prey — because knowledge is expensive.**

\---

## 23\. Final Vision

This game should feel like a co-op horror story that remembers the players even after they die. The hospital remembers. The Entity remembers. The dead remember. And the players who learn too much learn that the truth is not a reward — it is a cost.

**The Ward That Remembered** should stand out by combining a terrifying antagonist, a painful co-op structure, subtle lore, and an ending that forces human choice under impossible pressure.
